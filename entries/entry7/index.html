HALLOWEEN

Halloween has a long-standing association with candy, especially through the popular tradition of trick-or-treating. This practice, in which children go door-to-door asking for treats, has become almost synonymous with Halloween in many countries, particularly in the United States.

The connection between candy and Halloween can be traced back to the mid-20th century, when candy manufacturers began marketing their products specifically for the holiday. Before the 1950s, trick-or-treaters were given a variety of items, including coins, fruit, and homemade treats. However, concerns about food safety, coupled with the convenience of pre-packaged goods, led to candy becoming the dominant treat for Halloween.

Trick or Treat

Trick-or-Treating itself has roots in the Celtic tradition of Samhain, where people would dress up in costumes to ward off ghosts and spirits. In some early forms of trick-or-treating, people would go door-to-door in costume, performing tricks or songs in exchange for food or coins, which evolved into the modern exchange of candy.

Over the years, candy has come to symbolize the fun and indulgence of Halloween, with households giving out treats to children in festive packaging.
